A New Beginning, A Familiar Memory

On carrying the past forward into new chapters

14 November 2025 • 6 min read • Andreas Nyberg

This week I received a welcome pack for the role I start on Monday. Inside, among the standard items, was a small pot containing mint seeds. That single detail turned a routine moment into something more significant. Mint has been connected to the memory of someone I lost years ago, and finding it here, at the start of a new chapter, created an unexpected sense of continuity.

This is about how seemingly small things can carry weight, how memory travels with us, and what it means to move forward while honouring the people who shaped who we are.

A Gift That Meant More Than Expected

The welcome pack arrived with the usual contents: branded materials, an onboarding guide, practical information. The mint seeds were tucked in as a small gesture, likely chosen without particular thought. But for me, they landed differently.

Mint has always been tied to Will, a close friend I lost many years ago. He was the kind of person you only meet once if you are fortunate. Sharp, perceptive, endearingly grumpy. He had little patience for pretence and no interest in being told what he wanted to hear. If you asked for his opinion, you received it, unfiltered and direct.

That honesty made him difficult at times, but it also made him invaluable. He challenged assumptions, sharpened thinking, and refused to let mediocrity slide. He was also completely obsessed with plants.

Remembering Will

Will's dream was to work at Kew Gardens. Not as a visitor or occasional volunteer, but as someone embedded in the greenhouses and botanical collections, surrounded by the living systems he understood better than most people. He could talk for hours about soil chemistry, Latin classifications, and the specific conditions required to grow herbs most of us only recognise as ingredients.

Mint was his signature. He cultivated it everywhere: windowsills, garden beds, improvised containers in spaces that seemed too small to sustain anything green. He harvested it, dried it, brewed tea from it, and distributed bundles of it like a quiet evangelist. The scent of fresh mint still brings him back immediately.

After he passed, I discovered a bench at Kew Gardens dedicated to him. It sits in a quiet corner, surrounded by the greenery he loved. Every year, I visit. I sit. I spend a quiet moment there, marking another year since he left.

It has become a ritual, not out of obligation, but because it feels necessary. A way to honour someone who meant something, who influenced how I think, who set standards I still try to meet.

Mint as a Thread Through Time

There is also a playlist called Mint. It holds tracks we shared, argued about, and played too loudly in cars, bedrooms, and poorly lit pubs. Music has a particular ability to preserve memory in ways other things cannot.

A song plays, and suddenly you are back in a specific conversation, reliving a joke that should not still be funny, feeling understood by someone who saw through the noise. The playlist is not sacred. It simply exists, a reminder that some friendships extend beyond their original timelines.

The Mint Playlist

Listen to the Mint playlist on Spotify: a collection of tracks that defined a friendship, preserved a memory, and continue to bring Will back through sound.

A Tribute in Music

Another thread in this memory involves a band called Candy Apple Blue. They invited fans to submit photos of people they had lost, to be included in a music video for their song *Not A Day Goes By*. I sent in a picture of Will.

It became part of something larger: a tribute woven into a piece of art that reaches people across the world. Every time I watch that video, I see him there, frozen in a moment, part of a collective act of remembrance for people who mattered.

Candy Apple Blue: Not A Day Goes By

The band Candy Apple Blue created a space for their fans to honour lost loved ones by including submitted photos in their music video. Will's image became part of that tribute, a small but meaningful inclusion in a work that acknowledges grief and memory with care.

Beginning a New Chapter

When the mint seeds arrived in my welcome pack, they created a connection I had not anticipated. I do not believe in orchestrated cosmic signals, but I do think we notice patterns when we are ready to see them, when circumstances align in ways that feel meaningful.

The mint, the bench, the playlist, the music video, and now this pot of seeds have all become part of the same story. They tie themselves into this new chapter I am beginning.

I am joining a team and stepping into work that aligns with how I want to operate: structured, technically focused, and well-matched to what I do best. After years of navigating complex environments, building LT.Solutions, and managing the unpredictable demands of consultancy work, this feels like a deliberate move toward clarity and focus.

The coincidence with the mint seeds made it personal in a way I had not expected. Not mystical, but grounding. A reminder that you carry your past with you: the people, the influences, the standards, the stories. They do not disappear. They inform every next step.

You do not leave your friends behind when they are gone. You carry them forward in the choices you make, the standards you hold, and the moments you pause to remember.

Will would have interrogated this decision thoroughly. He would have asked direct questions about the role, challenged whether it was genuinely the right fit, then grudgingly conceded it sounded reasonable. That was how we worked.

Carrying Memory Into What Comes Next

This is the beginning of something new. It already feels grounded in what came before.

The mint will grow. The memories remain. The work ahead will be challenging and rewarding in ways I have not yet fully mapped. Somewhere in all of that, there is a thread connecting where I have been to where I am going.

New beginnings do not erase the past. They build on it. The people who shaped us, the lessons they taught, and the standards they set continue to inform how we move forward. That continuity matters. It grounds confidence, clarifies purpose, and reminds us why we set the bar where we do.

Will would tell me to stop overcomplicating it and water the plant. So I will.
